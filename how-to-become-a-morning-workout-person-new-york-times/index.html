If you're someone who struggles to stay consistent with your workout routine, becoming a morning workout person could be the solution you've been looking for. Not only does exercising in the morning set a positive tone for the rest of your day, but it also ensures that you get your workout in before any unexpected events or excuses arise. In this article, we'll explore tips from The New York Times on how to become a morning workout person.

Start Slow

One of the biggest mistakes people make when trying to become a morning workout person is going too hard too fast. According to The New York Times, it's important to ease into your new routine by starting with just a few days a week and gradually increasing the number of days as your body adjusts. This will not only help prevent injury but also make the transition to morning workouts more sustainable in the long term.

Prepare the Night Before

Another tip from The New York Times is to prepare for your morning workout the night before. This can include laying out your workout clothes, prepping your breakfast, and setting your alarm. By doing these things ahead of time, you'll set yourself up for success and make it easier to get out of bed in the morning.

Buddy System

Working out with a friend or partner can be a great way to stay motivated and accountable. According to The New York Times, finding a workout buddy who shares your goals and schedule can make all the difference when it comes to sticking to your morning routine. Not only will you have someone to hold you accountable, but you'll also have someone to share your progress and successes with.

Accountability

In addition to a workout buddy, there are other ways to hold yourself accountable when it comes to morning workouts. The New York Times suggests tracking your progress, whether it's through a fitness app, a journal, or simply a calendar. By seeing your progress over time, you'll be more motivated to stick to your routine.

Find a Workout You Enjoy

One of the biggest obstacles to working out in the morning is simply not enjoying the activity. According to The New York Times, finding a workout you enjoy is key to making morning workouts a sustainable habit. Whether it's running, yoga, or weightlifting, finding an activity you look forward to can make all the difference.

Listen to Your Body

While it's important to stay consistent with your morning workouts, it's also important to listen to your body. According to The New York Times, if you're feeling overly tired or sore, it's okay to take a rest day or switch up your routine. Pushing yourself too hard can lead to injury and burnout, so it's important to find a balance that works for you.

Nutrition

Finally, The New York Times reminds us that proper nutrition is key to making the most of your morning workouts. Eating a balanced breakfast that includes protein and complex carbohydrates can help fuel your workout and ensure that you have the energy to get through your day. Additionally, staying hydrated is important for both your workout and overall health.

FAQ

What time should I wake up for a morning workout?

The ideal wake-up time for a morning workout varies depending on your schedule and preferences. However, The New York Times suggests waking up at least an hour before your workout to give your body time to wake up and properly prepare for exercise.

What should I eat before a morning workout?

Eating a balanced breakfast that includes protein and complex carbohydrates can help fuel your morning workout. This could include oatmeal with fruit and nuts, a smoothie with protein powder, or eggs with whole grain toast.

What if I can't stick to my morning workout routine?

It's okay if you can't stick to your morning workout routine every day. The New York Times suggests being flexible and finding a routine that works for you. That could mean working out in the afternoon or evening instead, or simply adjusting your schedule to allow for more morning workouts over time.

Final Thoughts

Becoming a morning workout person takes time and effort, but it can have a huge impact on your overall health and well-being. By starting slow, preparing the night before, finding a workout buddy, and staying accountable, you can make morning workouts a sustainable habit. Additionally, listening to your body, finding a workout you enjoy, and fueling your body with proper nutrition can help you get the most out of your workouts. With these tips from The New York Times, you'll be on your way to becoming a morning workout person in no time.
